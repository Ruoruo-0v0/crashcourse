October 25th Workshops

Chrome Extensions | Karan Sikka

What can you do with chrome extensions? Replace "the cloud" with "my butt" on all web pages. Replace all images with smiley faces. Analyze browser history for fun and profit. P2P screen-sharing. The possibilities are nearly endless.
In this talk we'll learn what a chrome extension can (and can't) do, how the architecture works, and some hypothetical examples of how you'd build a chrome extension.
Come with crazy ideas, and leave with a deeper understanding of Chrome and its extensions.
Prerequisites:
A basic understanding of web development
Additional Resources:
N/A


Intro to CSS Frameworks | Alvin Wang

In this class you will learn about what CSS frameworks are and how they can be used to create well-designed, responsive websites. We will look at the components that make up a CSS Framework and learn how to use one in a web project. In particular, we will look at Foundation and use it in demo to see its magic in action.
Prerequisites:
A rough understanding of HTML
Additional Resources:
Laptop


Intro to Web Security | Ryan Flood

With the internet being the biggest target for modern development efforts, applications have quickly become large scale ubiquitous systems. One of the problems with reaching out to the internet however is that with the hordes of good users, you will also get the bad. This talk will discuss the most common attack methods against web applications, and go through a hands-on demo of what you can do with them. We will also discuss best practices for securing your apps and your system as a whole.
Prerequisites:
N/A

How To Win Every Hackathon (Or, Why Hackathons Aren’t About Winning) | Avi Romanoff & Megan Chen

It seems everybody’s doing them these days. From designers to scientists to programmers and everyone in between, people of all backgrounds are finding tremendous value (and fun!) in attending weekend-long collaborative project-building competitions. With 50+ hackathons planned for this year, there has never been a better time to get involved. Hear from veteran hackathon attendees about their experiences, insights, and tips for making the most of your hackathon experience, whether it’s your first or your fifteenth. We’ll talk about ideation, team-forming, balancing school-work and hackathons, what judges look for, what hackathons you should attend (and why), and how to maximize the learning, friend-making, and fun-having. ... and maybe even sleep.
Prerequisites:
Absolutely nothing! You don’t need to be a computer science major or even have programmed before. This talk is for everyone — from veteran hackathon hackers to curious students.
Additional Resources:
Major League Hacking: http://mlh.io

Intro to Web Applications | Andrew Benson

Open Source & Desktop Linux Development for Fun and Profit | Avi Romanoff

Do you know what and who it takes to build a modern Linux distribution? What languages, frameworks, tools, and skills are needed? Do you want to get involved in an open source project, but you don’t know where to find one or how to get involved? Are you a veteran hacker looking for a some cool projects to work on (and maybe even earn some $$$ on the side)? Are you a novice looking for a great way to get some real-world experience? If you answered “yes” to any of these questions, this talk is for you! Hear from a 5-year veteran of the Linux desktop and open source communities about what makes the free software community tick, the tools of the trade, and how you can get involved. Write your very own native app for elementary OS using the Vala programming language — no prior experience needed!

Nothing, but a rough understanding of what Linux is and basic programming skills would be helpful (though not essential). Non-CS majors are very much welcome!

elementary OS: elementaryos.org

Advanced Computer Security | Max Serrano & Ryan Goulden

Heard of Heartbleed, Shellshock, Sandworm, or POODLE? Or maybe of blind SQL injection, cross-site request forgery, or hash extension? In this talk, we’ll be talking about how attacks like the above happen, and how to use and avoid them. We’ll be talking about subtle mistakes that lead to catastrophic vulnerabilities, and you’ll be able to play around with them!
Prerequisites:
Some understanding of web requests (so, cookies, GET and POST parameters, and that’s about it)
Have seen Python before
Some understanding of databases (have you seen SQL before? you’re set)
Additional Resources:
You may want to bring a laptop! It’s totally optional, but we’ll have setups running where you can play around with some of the bugs we describe.

Intro to Cookies & Authentication | Andrew Benson

Ever wanted to learn how to bake authentic chocolate-chip cookies? Then this crashcourse might not be for you. Instead, we’re going to learn about internet cookies, the mechanism that powers login systems, ad tracking, and shopping carts across the web. We’ll briefly explore how to exploit cookies on other websites for your own purposes, and then we’ll attempt to build our own login system on a website that is safe from basic cookie vulnerabilities. By the end, you’ll understand how cookies work and you’ll know why websites can only reset your password instead of telling you what it is.
Prerequisites:
The ability to SSH into Andrew Linux and use the terminal there.
Basic knowledge of Python (15-110/15-112 is sufficient).
Basic understanding of HTTP and web servers strongly recommended.
Additional Resources:
Bring a laptop


Taking Full Advantage of the Vim Text Editor | Oscar Bezi

As a developer, there is a lot of value in using a full featured text editor. Vim is one such editor, and I will be going over how to make the most of it. While it has a higher entry barrier than Sublime or Gedit, using Vim has a great many advantages (which I'll also talk about!). I'll begin with a brief overview of how it works, how to configure it to suit your needs, and then go into more advanced topics like advanced movement, commands, and installing plugins. I'm not here to convince you Vim is the best text editor, but hopefully you'll be feel that way from using it like a 'power user'!
Prerequisites:
A working understanding of HTML/CSS. No design knowledge required, but some background in design will make the talk more effective.
